Rules and respect

Introduction

Essential rules to follow for respectful dining in Japanese culture.

Do not stick chopsticks upright in rice

This resembles a funeral ritual called tsukitate-bashi, where chopsticks are placed upright in a bowl of rice as an offering to the dead. Doing this at the table is considered extremely disrespectful.

Avoid pointing or playing with chopsticks

Gesturing, waving, or passing food directly from chopstick to chopstick mimics funeral customs and is seen as rude or careless.

Why lifting a knife can be seen as rude

In traditional Japanese dining, knives are not typically used at the table, as most dishes are prepared in bite-sized portions with chopsticks in mind. Lifting or using a knife may be interpreted as aggressive or as a sign that the kitchen didn’t properly prepare the food.

Say “Itadakimasu” before eating

This customary phrase, meaning “I humbly receive,” is a way to show gratitude for the food, the people who prepared it, and the life taken to provide it. Not saying it can be seen as ungrateful.

Eat quietly, but slurping noodles is acceptable

While talking with a full mouth or chewing loudly is impolite, slurping noodles (like ramen or soba) is culturally accepted and can even signal that you're enjoying the meal. So yes—the louder slurp may be interpreted as greater appreciation, especially in casual settings.
